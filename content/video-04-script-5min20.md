## 2:00 – 2:50 | Who is Anil

> Anil is one of the finest science writers today — *Nature*, *New Scientist*, *Quanta*.
>
> But he did not write from the outside. He watched the same lecture series that stopped me on that hill. He learned Python with MIT students. He interviewed leading researchers — and he makes himself a student in the book, not a guru.
>
> Geoffrey Hinton called it a masterpiece. After reading it, I understand why.

---

## 2:50 – 3:40 | What makes it different

> Most ML books fall into two traps.
>
> **Oversimplification** — warm analogies that collapse when you push on them.
>
> **Or overwhelming math** from page one — as if you were supposed to already know everything.
>
> *Why Machines Learn* does neither. It builds understanding the way real learning happens: repetition, layers, honest revisiting.
>
> At the end of the prologue Anil writes:
>
> *"I hope your neurons enjoy this process as much as mine did."*
>
> That line stayed with me for weeks.

---

## 3:40 – 5:00 | Idea 1 — patterns (ducklings)

> Here is the first idea that rewired my thinking.
>
> A baby bird does not memorize its mother. It learns a *relationship* — stay close to what moves first. Lorenz showed goslings will follow *you* if you are first. They learn *similarity itself* — and use it in new situations later.
>
> A tiny brain. A few days old. Doing abstraction.
>
> A neural network on cats does not memorize ten thousand photos. It learns what features mean "cat-ness" — and generalizes.
>
> **Machine learning did not invent a new intelligence. It formalized something nature perfected for hundreds of millions of years.**
